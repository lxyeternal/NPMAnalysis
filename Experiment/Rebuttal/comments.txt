Review #2381A
===========================================================================

Overall merit
-------------
2. Weak reject

Paper summary
-------------
Ecosystems like NPM are widely used in modern software engineering, which makes them easy targets for malicious actors that can inject malicious code into the packages. Many tools have been proposed to prevent these types of attacks, which feature vastly different techniques and approaches to detect the bad packages. Individually, all of them have been evaluated before, but an in-depth comparison on common ground has been missing so far. The paper heads out to improve evaluation standards in that field. After establishing a substantial dataset of benign and malicious packages, the paper analyses tool reports to create a taxonomy of attacks and evasion strategies. Using the dataset and the taxonomy, an empirical study reports on the effectiveness of popular analysis tools. Results show that stark variances exist between the tools, which also depend on the type of evasion strategy that is applied by the malicious package. Finally, a temporal analysis shows that especially the detection quality of ML-based approaches falls off after time.

Strengths
---------
- Novelty: Novel study and useful dataset
- Rigor: Overall, the methodology of the paper is sound
- Relevance: Important research, interesting findings
- Presentation: Well written paper, good structure

Weaknesses
----------
- Rigor: The methodology has several minor flaws.
- Rigor: I am very concerned about the impact of time in the overall study design.
- Relevance: Why is the taxonomy not treated/released as a main contribution?
- Relevance: Lack of discussion reduces the paper to a collection of statistics
- Presentation: Writing rough in the end, figures hard to read.

Detailed comments for authors
-----------------------------
### Comments on Novelty

- The presented research is novel. I am not aware of a similar large scale comparison of tools and especially the availability of dataset and taxonomy is a contribution for the field.

### Comments on Rigor


- I generally agree with methodology for the Taxonomy creation... the paper never introduces the resulting taxonomy though and just assumes readers are familiar with the classification types, which are then just used later, for example, in Figure 2 and Figure 3. A short elaboration on the various categories would have been useful for me. As is, it is difficult to follow the findings of RQ2, as the terms do not say anything to me. The taxonomy should have been treated as a contribution and presented accordingly (and not just used as a means).

- The code snippet normalization followed by md5 calculation will fail to identify slight variations. I would recommend to apply alpha conversion first to get rid of identifiers and then apply an n-gram hashing (e.g., winnowing) to calculate similarity between two snippets.

- The overall methodology is straightforward and seems to be sound at the presented level of detail... that being said, the paper is omitting various details that would be crucial for verification and transparency (see below).

- It is questionable that the automated mapping of the 19k summaries has not been (at least partially) validated to show reliability of the mapping strategy.

- I am concerned how the temporal aspect of security influences the paper, especially the selection of benign packages during the dataset creation and the temporal analysis in RQ3. In the current methodology, a "benign package" is not guaranteed to be free of malicious code, it has just not been raised by any detection tool... the issue with security concerns is that they only pop up when they get detected or exploited, so there is usually a delay involved. I would have preferred to avoid packages in the benign dataset that are newer than 6 months and only focused on 2020-24 in the temporal analysis.

### Comments on Relevance

- The paper contributes to a highly relevant domain. It is of utmost importance to secure release repositories like NPN, which represent the modern infrastructure that the society relies on. Improving the evaluation standards is a great step in this direction.

- The three RQs give a useful overview over the various tools and nicely introduces and discusses the advantages and disadvantages of the various tools through examples. I would have found it interesting to contrast these findings with the original claims that have been made about these tools in their original publications.

- Both throughout the paper and especially in the related work section, the paper collects a good number of closely related works.

- The paper lacks a direly needed discussion section. After establishing the taxonomy, a thorough empirical study is conducted. Unfortunately, all results are only presented in a very descriptive manner and leave me as a reader asking me about the relevance of these findings. Are there recommendations for future work that go beyond a request for improved evaluation through standardized benchmarks? I would have hoped that the discussion would pick up these individual findings, set them in context, and sketch the problems that can be solved by future work in the field. Without such an outlook, the paper lost an opportunity to justify its relevance for others.

- I applaud the authors for the research done and for the efforts to improve evaluations in the field. I think the study as a whole and its accompanying artifacts is very relevant, but the paper does not reflect this this yet. The manuscript makes the impression that, in the end, the time ran out to present the key findings. I would like to highly encourage the authors to revise the paper and identify the take home message(s) of the paper that should be heard by others, this will catapult the paper onto a whole different level.


### Comments on Verifiability and Transparency

- The creation of the dataset is broken down into many steps, but often the details were left out, likely to save space. These details would have been important for verification though. For example, what was the methodology for the screening of the two people that was performed to verify the malicious findings? Also, it is unclear to me how the "3-5 core keywords" have been extracted from the generated summaries, as is, it does not sound like a repeatable methodology.

- I do not understand how the keyword matching for the category identification worked. I would imagine that it is very difficult to match arbitrary texts given that a simple typo could break a simple string based matching. The paper should expand on this part.

- The paper contains a link to an online appendix hosted as a Google Site. While Code and data are hosted separately (And seem to be complete), I am strongly asking the authors to include data and code in a repository that guarantees long term archival, such as zenodo.org. If have already experienced that artifacts have vanished from websites after publication, so I am treating publications on proprietary hosters as *informative,* but *non-existing* from the perspective of reproducibility.

### Comments on Presentation

- The writing in the first half of the paper is excellent and was easy to follow. The quality drops off in the second half of the evaluation though. Suddenly, figures are no longer properly elaborated in the text and it generally becomes harder to follow.

- All tables in the paper are well formatted, elaborated on in the paper, and useful for understanding the presented observations and research results.

- The font size is too small across all figures to be legible. These pictures should also not be included as pixel graphics, but in scalable formats like pdf or svg... not just to decrease file size of the resulting paper, but mostly to improve readability.

- Typo: p5: "evidence.While" (missing space)

Questions for authors’ response
-------------------------------
I have no questions that would change my assessment. I invite the authors to point out mistakes in my review, if they want to respond.



Review #2381B
===========================================================================

Overall merit
-------------
2. Weak reject

Paper summary
-------------
This paper provides a large-scale empirical benchmark of malicious npm package detection, evaluating six tools across static, dynamic, ML, and LLM-based paradigms on a curated dataset of 13,571 packages. The study offers comprehensive comparisons, a taxonomy of behaviors and evasion techniques.

Strengths
---------
- Tackles a timely and practically important problem with high relevance to software supply chain security.

- Provides a large dataset and standardized evaluation

Weaknesses
----------
- Limited novelty: primarily an evaluation study; prior works benchmarked subsets of tools.

- Dataset concerns: inherits biases from prior sources, manual labeling lacks inter-rater reliability reporting.

- Tool coverage and practicality: excludes some state-of-the-art tools; little discussion of runtime cost or deployment feasibility.

Detailed comments for authors
-----------------------------
Overall, this study is a useful consolidation of knowledge but mainly an evaluation contribution with limited methodological novelty.

**Novelty**.
The originality of this study lies in breadth and systematization - bringing together static, dynamic, ML, and LLM detectors in a single standardized evaluation. The taxonomy of behaviors and evasion techniques adds structure to the analysis. The contribution still is incremental compared to prior efforts like Amalfi [1] and SpiderScan [2], which already benchmarked detectors at a smaller scale.
The paper does not offer new detection algorithms, frameworks, or analysis techniques. ICSE expects more conceptual advances. As written, this reads more as a benchmark report than a research breakthrough.

[1] https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3510003.3510104

[2] https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3691620.3695492

**Rigor**.
The study uses a standardized dataset, consistent metrics (precision, recall, F1, temporal analysis), and carefully documents the evaluation setup. The inclusion of behavioral analysis, evasion coverage, and temporal robustness shows commendable depth.
However, the dataset construction process is problematic. It merges prior datasets, which risks duplication and inherited bias. Manual validation is done by two annotators, but no inter-rater agreement is reported. That raises questions about label reliability. The policy of treating timeouts (>300s) as benign unfairly penalizes analysis-heavy tools like Packj, biasing results toward lighter detectors. Without strong evidence of dataset quality and fair handling of runtime issues, the rigor of the comparisons is undermined.

**Relevance**.
This work is highly relevant to ICSE. Malicious npm packages affect millions of developers and organizations, and knowing which tools are reliable is both academically and practically valuable.
In terms of impact, the paper contribution is mainly infrastructural: it provides a dataset, a taxonomy, and benchmark numbers that others can build on. This is useful, but its scientific significance is moderate. The findings confirm some expectations - static tools are stable, ML tools decay, combining approaches helps, without breaking much new ground. The dataset is also frozen in time, and without a clear plan for ongoing updates, it risks becoming outdated quickly as the npm ecosystem evolves.

**Presentation**.
The paper is clearly structured. The main findings are easy to follow, and the taxonomy of behaviors/evasions is intuitive. Dataset construction is described in dense text; a visual pipeline would help. Claims like state of the art are overstated. False positives and false negatives are reported in aggregate but not illustrated with concrete examples.

**Verifiability & Transparency**.
The paper commits to releasing the dataset, taxonomy, and evaluation scripts, which makes replication possible in principle.
Some tools (like SocketAI) depend on closed, evolving LLM APIs. This means results may change over time and exact reproduction is fragile.
The dataset is frozen in time; npm attacks evolve rapidly, so without a refresh mechanism, results may quickly become outdated. Long-term reproducibility is doubtful. Even if the dataset is released, API-driven tools and evolving models will prevent exact replication.
The transparency around dataset validation is weak since no inter-rater agreement is reported for manual labeling.

Questions for authors’ response
-------------------------------
1. How did you ensure label quality in the dataset (e.g., was inter-rater agreement measured)?

2. Why were timeouts treated as benign rather than indeterminate, and how does this affect results?

3. What benign behaviors trigger Packj’s high false positive rate, and can you categorize them?

4. Why do ML tools degrade over time - dataset drift, outdated features, or retraining gaps?

This paper proposes a large-scale empirical study on the performance of malicious package detection on the npm ecosystem.

# Novelty 
This paper makes a novel contribution by doing a large-scale comparison of existing npm-based malicious package detection tools. The dataset size is comprehensive, and the selected tools represent a broad category of tools, such as static analyzer, dynamic analyzer, and ML-based. However, one notable LLM based detector was not included: J. Wang et al., "MalPacDetector: An LLM-Based Malicious NPM Package Detector," in IEEE Transactions on Information Forensics and Security, vol. 20, pp. 6279-6291, 2025, doi: 10.1109/TIFS.2025.3580336

# Relevance
The work is very relevant in the sense that this comparison will inform practitioners on what tools to use in practical cases. 

# Presentation
The paper is mostly easy to follow; however, several part of the paper, such as sections 2.3 and 3.2.1, needs rework. Section 2.3 does not specify why the authors need a taxonomy, nor how it was built. 

# Verifiability & transparency
Except for section 2.3, the rest of the paper reads logically coherent, and the steps are reproducible. 

# Rigor
There are several weak points:
(a) How the taxonomy of behavior is constructed is not clear. The authors mentioned they are using an LLM; however, there is not much detail about it
(b) The taxonomy was built primarily from the output from GuardDog; however, GuardDog itself was one of the selected tools for comparison. Hence, in Table 3, GuardDog performs way better in f1 score compared to others
(c) RQ2 findings should include several illustrations or case studies, so that readers can see exactly in what cases certain tools perform better or worse. Especially, some explicit examples of false positives could add value to the findings
(d) RQ2 methodology was also not clear. How the behavior-based comparison was done? 
(e) How was the detection rate calculated? What was the extent of false positives in RQ2 across different behaviors?

Cycle-2-Rebuttal Response by Author [Wenbo Guo <honywenair@gmail.com>] (1464 words)
---------------------------------------------------------------------------
We highly appreciate all reviewers’ comments.

***Taxonomy Construction Issues (RA, RC)*** 

We thank the reviewers for these important concerns regarding our taxonomy construction methodology. Our systematic approach follows a five-step pipeline: (1) GuardDog identifies suspicious code locations, (2) LLM extracts and validates malicious code context, (3) LLM generates behavioral summaries for each context, (4) TF-IDF analysis of all summaries extracts the most representative keywords, (5) keyword based matching classifies behaviors into our 15 category framework.

Our taxonomy covers 15 behaviors: System Reconnaissance (4,895 packages) involves gathering OS version, hardware details, and network configuration; Command Execution (5,167 packages) executes system commands and scripts. Other categories include Data Exfiltration, Network Communication, Obfuscation Techniques, and specialized attacks like Prototype Pollution, a JavaScript specific vulnerability. Complete behavioral descriptions and distributions are available on our website \[1\].

This methodology ensures reliable classification because LLM summaries normalize diverse code implementations into consistent terminology, and TF-IDF identifies statistically significant behavioral indicators rather than arbitrary keywords. Manual validation of 500 classifications achieved 97.4% accuracy (487 correct, 13 misclassifications). Regarding circularity concerns, GuardDog serves only for initial code localization—all behavioral analysis relies on LLM interpretation, making our taxonomy tool agnostic.

***Limited Methodological Novelty (RB)***

This work conducts a comprehensive empirical study to systematically analyze existing npm malicious package detection tools—a critical evaluation that has been lacking in the field. Prior works (Amalfi, SpiderScan) evaluated 2-3 tools on \<1,000 samples without behavioral, evasion, or temporal analysis. Our contributions: (1) first standardized evaluation across 9 variants on 6,547 packages, the largest npm malicious package dataset, (2) systematic behavioral/evasion analysis revealing detection blind spots, (3) temporal degradation analysis demonstrating ML limitations, (4) validated combination strategies achieving 94.9% accuracy.

***Lack of Analysis & discussion (RA, RB, RC)***

We acknowledge the reviewers' concerns about insufficient analytical depth. Our analysis reveals concrete patterns: False positive root causes: GuardDog's 153 false positives stem primarily from npm-install-script triggers (71.90%) flagging legitimate build processes. GuardDog uses Semgrep static rules with taint analysis to track execution flows, resulting in lower false positives. In contrast, OSSGadget's 5,893 false positives are dominated by LOLBAS.Linux detections (84.01%) and Windows patterns (64.27%) because it employs regex-based matching that only checks for command name presence without analyzing context or usage patterns, failing to filter legitimate uses. Our revision will provide comprehensive analysis including detailed root cause breakdowns, behavioral detection capability explanations, and tool complementarity insights to inform future research directions and practical deployment decisions.

***False Positive Analysis (RC)***

We analyzed Packj's false positive patterns across both analysis modes. Static analysis relies on 343 sensitive API patterns, with process operations (21%), file operations (19%), and network operations (16.9%) triggering most false positives. Dynamic analysis performs strace logging during installation with rule-based matching. All 4,520 false positive packages contained process, file, and network operations, averaging 770.5 network operations per package.

Packj's coarse-grained pattern matching cannot distinguish legitimate administrative functions from malicious system access. This aggressive heuristic approach achieves high recall but generates substantial false positives in production deployments. We will provide detailed false positive case studies and in-depth analysis in our revision.

***Dataset Construction Issues (RA, RB)***

We thank the reviewers for raising this concern. We constructed multi-source datasets to achieve the most comprehensive evaluation possible, maximizing coverage across diverse temporal periods and malicious behaviors while maintaining consistent preprocessing standards. For benign packages, we selected popular packages because their high download volumes ensure extensive scrutiny by security companies and the community, providing reliable benign classification. Additionally, popular packages' complex functionality and structure effectively expose different tools' false positive patterns, unlike simple packages with minimal code. We will also add analysis of benign packages older than 6 months and focus on 2020-2024 temporal analysis in our revision.

***Inter-rater Reliability Missing (RB, RC)***

We thank the reviewers for raising this concern. Our manual validation involved two security experts independently evaluating packages using established guidelines (obfuscated code patterns, malicious IoCs, suspicious API sequences, VirusTotal cross-referencing, etc.). Results showed exceptional consistency: 7,764 packages (99.7%) had unanimous agreement, with only 20 packages (0.3%) showing disagreement. Disagreement cases were resolved using GitHub Advisory and OSV databases as authoritative sources. This systematic approach achieved Cohen's Kappa \= 0.971, indicating excellent inter-rater reliability. The extremely low disagreement rate reflects that npm malicious packages typically contain obvious indicators easily recognizable by experienced analysts.


***Tool Evaluation Bias (RB, RC)***

We appreciate the reviewer for pointing this out. We extended timeout limits to 600 seconds and re-evaluated the dataset. Only 56 packages (0.41% of 13,571) exceeded this extended timeout. Among these, 34 were benign packages and 22 were malicious packages. Packj\_trace achieved F1-score of 71.76%, nearly identical to the original 71.81% performance.

Timeouts occur when packages have complex structures and extensive dependencies. Benign packages with intricate build processes require longer processing during dynamic analysis. This pattern shows timeout handling does not systematically bias results against analysis-intensive tools. Complete Packj dynamic analysis results are available on our website for transparency.

***GuardDog Circularity (RC)***

Thanks for raising this issue. Our cost-effective approach uses GuardDog to identify suspicious file locations, then applies LLM analysis to extract malicious code context from these targeted regions. For packages GuardDog cannot identify, we perform comprehensive file-by-file analysis—only 688 packages required this complete analysis. We manually validated the extracted contexts from all these packages to ensure accuracy, guaranteeing taxonomy reliability independent of GuardDog's detection capabilities.

***Runtime Cost and Deployment Feasibility (RB)***

We acknowledge the lack of runtime cost analysis and conducted additional experiments to address this concern. We selected 1,000 packages from our datasets for runtime evaluation. Packages averaged 47.4 files and 2,961.8 lines of code.

Results show substantial performance differences across tools. GuardDog processes packages fastest at 2.55 seconds per package. OSSGadget requires 7.94 seconds per package. SAP\_XGB needs 9.58 seconds per package due to feature extraction overhead. Packj\_trace demands 17.90 seconds per package for dynamic analysis and installation monitoring. SocketAI requires 146.34 seconds per package because it queries the LLM 17 times per file. Our evaluation used GPT-4.1-mini and GPT-4.1-nano models for faster processing. Alternative models would incur higher computational costs.

***RQ2 Behavioral Analysis Methodology (RC)***

We thank the reviewer for pointing out this methodological question. We acknowledge that our paper omitted the detailed RQ2 methodology. We followed the behavioral comparison approach from the ASE'23 paper, where all malicious code summary behaviors constitute the package's complete behavioral profile. When a tool successfully detects a package, we attribute detection capability to all behaviors present in that package, with each behavior type counted only once per package. Detection rates are calculated by counting behavioral instances in detected packages versus total behavioral instances per category across the dataset. This package level attribution provides systematic analysis of tool capabilities across our 15 behavioral categories, revealing detection patterns and blind spots. Complete behavioral breakdowns are available in our supplementary materials.


***Response to ML Tool Temporal Degradation (RB)***

Thanks for raising this issue. ML tool degradation stems from architectural limitations: SAP employs 126 features with 89 focused on file extension statistics rather than code semantics. Source code analysis lacks behavioral features, using only pattern matching—base64 detection, fixed IP/path prefixes, and hardcoded string matching from historical data. This creates two vulnerabilities: detection fails when patterns evolve, and cannot recognize semantically equivalent but syntactically different malicious behaviors. String\_Obfuscation increased from 16.3% to 22.2% (2021-2024), exploiting these static patterns and explaining SAP's decline from 86% to 39% detection rates. We will provide deeper analysis in our revision.

***Code Normalization Issues (RA)***

Our MD5 based normalization serves solely for duplicate detection to avoid redundant analysis. Attackers often batch deploy identical malicious code, so exact matching enables rapid identification of recurring patterns.

***Verifiability & Reproducibility (RA, RB, RC)***

We thank the reviewers for emphasizing these reproducibility concerns. We have reorganized our codebase with comprehensive documentation and step-by-step instructions, preserving all intermediate results to ensure full reproducibility of all tools and experimental results. All code and data will be migrated to Zenodo for long-term archival. We provide detailed instructions on our website \[1\].

***Writing and Presentation Quality (RA, RB, RC)***

We acknowledge the presentation concerns regarding figure readability and writing quality. In our revision, we will redesign all figures with improved fonts and scalable formats, and enhance the overall manuscript clarity.

Metareview
----------
The reviewers thank authors for submitting their work to ICSE and then responding to the reviewers. All reviewers agree with the relevance of the work and encourage its continuation and resubmission of the paper. The reviewers hope that the extensive and constructive reviews will help in revising the paper. The details are stated below.

(a) The identified taxonomy should have been treated as a contribution, but it was not written accordingly. The issue was also not sufficiently addressed in the rebuttal.

(b) The code normalization issue was not sufficiently addressed in the rebuttal. The reviewer suggested an alternative; however, the author did not address that in the rebuttal.

(c) The validity and reliability of the 19K automated summary mapping have not been discussed in the rebuttal.

(d) The paper heavily relies on the numerical findings of the benchmark; however, it lacks a discussion on the impact and future research pathways of these findings. Overall, the qualitative takeaways derived from these numbers are lacking and not sufficiently addressed in the rebuttal.

(e) The process for identifying two people to verify the findings related to the malicious package is not discussed in the rebuttal.

(f) The process for identifying the top 3-5 keywords, as well as the method used for keyword matching, was not discussed in the rebuttal.

(g) The paper does not offer new detection algorithms, frameworks, or analysis techniques. ICSE expects more conceptual advances. As written, this reads more as a benchmark report than a research breakthrough.

(h) The dataset was based on prior research endeavors; however, the dataset quality was not sufficiently evaluated or discussed. Although the authors mentioned interrater agreements in rebuttal, no sufficient discussion was given on how they were selected, how runtime issues are handled, etc. Furthermore, the dataset is primarily based on the outputs of existing tools, which creates a potential issue of false negatives that may introduce bias into the dataset, as discussed in the rebuttal.

(i) In the rebuttal, the authors also could not justify that they primarily used output from static analyzers and then used LLMs to verify. Therefore, the authors' contribution remains unclear, and an LLM verifies the dataset.
